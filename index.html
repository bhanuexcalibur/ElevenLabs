01 / Messaging Audit

What the current narrative
gets right, and where it leaks

ElevenLabs has strong product credibility. The gap is in enterprise storytelling: the existing messaging assumes buyers already believe AI voice is ready. Most enterprise buyers still don't.

Core positioning gap

The product story starts at the feature, not the problem

ElevenLabs leads with model quality: latency numbers, language counts, voice expressiveness. These are real advantages. But enterprise buyers don't buy models. They buy outcomes. A VP of CX at Deutsche Telekom isn't evaluating sub-100ms latency, she's evaluating whether her team can stop hemorrhaging customers to hold queues without tanking CSAT. The messaging needs to start at her desk, not at the benchmark.

Current framing
"Sub-100ms latency across 70+ languages with the most expressive AI voice model."
Reframe
"Hold queues are customer-churn machines. ElevenLabs agents answer on the first ring, handle the full conversation, and update the CRM before the customer hangs up."
What works

Voice quality is a genuine, defensible moat

Competitors like Vapi and Retell route to ElevenLabs TTS themselves. That's not a coincidence; it's validation. The synthesis stack being the industry standard that rivals build on is a credibility claim worth making loudly in enterprise sales cycles.

Needs sharpening

Enterprise proof lives in press releases, not the product page

Deutsche Telekom, Square, Revolut, and the Ukrainian Government are real, impressive customers. But their stories are buried in funding announcements. The product marketing needs to pull outcome metrics front and center: call containment rates, cost-per-call reductions, CSAT lifts. Those are the numbers that get budget approved.

Needs sharpening

Platform vs point-solution framing is unclear

ElevenAgents, ElevenCreative, ElevenAPI: the three-platform structure is logical internally but confusing in market. Enterprise buyers land on the homepage and can't immediately tell whether this is a voice API, an agent builder, or something bigger. A clearer platform narrative with defined entry points by buyer type would reduce churn at the top of funnel.

Missing entirely

Security and compliance aren't visible before the sales call

SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA, PCI DSS L1, GDPR, zero-retention mode, VPC deployment. ElevenLabs has the enterprise compliance stack. But none of it appears on the main product page. For a regulated-industry buyer doing early research, this creates a quiet disqualification before a salesperson ever gets involved.

03 / GTM Strategy

How I'd build the
enterprise narrative

ElevenLabs is in the middle of a platform transition: from best-in-class TTS API to full enterprise agent infrastructure. The PMM work that matters most right now is category definition, not feature marketing.

01
Category play

Own "Conversational Voice AI" before competitors define it for you

The voice AI market is fragmenting fast. Retell, Vapi, Bland, and Synthflow are all carving niches. ElevenLabs has a window to define the category rather than fight for position inside it. That means publishing the research, hosting the events, and setting the benchmarks that the industry references, not just wins. The Summit was a start. Make it annual, make it the authoritative event, and ensure every press output from it advances a category definition that ElevenLabs naturally wins.

Category creation Analyst relations Thought leadership
02
ICP clarity

Lead with verticals where voice is a revenue line, not a cost center

Financial services, telecom, and healthcare are the three enterprise verticals where voice AI generates measurable ROI fastest: collections calls, appointment reminders, member intake. Landing pages, case studies, and sales collateral need to speak in vertical-specific language rather than generic platform copy. "AI agents for contact centers" means nothing. "Cut collections call volume by 40% without adding headcount" means something to a CFO at a regional bank.

Sales enablement

Give enterprise reps the objection playbook, not just the pitch deck

Sales enablement that addresses these directly, with pre-built proof points and demo scripts rather than feature slides, shortens the evaluation cycle. ElevenLabs has the answers; the PMM job is packaging them to land in the first call.

06 / Competitive Positioning

Not a feature comparison.
A fight selection strategy.

The mistake most PMMs make in voice AI is building a comparison table and calling it a competitive strategy. The real question isn't "where do we beat Retell on specs?" It's "which deals should ElevenLabs enter, which should it pass on, and what category does it need to own to justify an $11B valuation?"

Compete hard

Branded, enterprise-grade inbound voice at global scale

When a Deutsche Telekom or a global bank needs an agent that sounds like it belongs to their brand, handles 30+ languages without routing to fallback, and has the compliance certifications to pass a procurement review, ElevenLabs wins. Retell is developer-first. Vapi requires assembling your own stack. Neither has the voice quality benchmark that makes a CX leader comfortable putting it in front of a million customers. This is the category ElevenLabs should be aggressively pricing to own.

Signals in the deal: Fortune 500, international footprint, brand-sensitive use case, inbound CX or support
Compete selectively

High-volume outbound calling at aggressive per-minute cost

Bland AI built specifically for outbound campaign volume: collections, reminders, sales dial lists. Their architecture handles thousands of concurrent outbound calls at pricing that rewards volume. ElevenLabs can win here on voice quality, but shouldn't enter this on price. The ICP for ElevenLabs is a company where voice quality and brand fidelity justify a premium, not one optimizing for the lowest cost-per-dial. Competing in outbound burns margin and attracts the wrong customer profile for the platform story.

Signals to pass: SMB outbound campaigns, price-sensitive dialer replacement, no brand differentiation requirement
Don't fight here

Full CCaaS replacement against Cognigy and Genesys incumbents

Cognigy and Genesys are deeply embedded in enterprise contact center infrastructure: workforce management, omnichannel routing, historical analytics, and years of custom workflow logic. Displacing them requires a 12-18 month procurement cycle and a full-stack CCaaS pitch. ElevenLabs wins as the voice layer that makes those platforms sound better, not as a replacement. The right motion with Cognigy-entrenched accounts is a partnership or embedded integration, not a platform displacement play.

Signals to redirect: Existing Genesys/Cisco/Cognigy contracts, complex IVR migration requirements, full contact center RFP
Own the category

The infrastructure layer that makes enterprise voice AI real

Here's the positioning opportunity most voice AI companies miss: ElevenLabs is already the de facto infrastructure standard. Retell, Vapi, and Bland all integrate ElevenLabs TTS when they want calls to sound good. That's not a coincidence to bury in a blog post; it's the category claim that defines the market. The PMM work is making that fact loud: ElevenLabs is to enterprise voice what Stripe is to payments, the layer everyone builds on, and the one enterprise buyers should procure directly to cut out the intermediary.

08 / Sales Artifact

An actual battlecard,
not a slide about battlecards

This is what a working ElevenLabs Agents objection-handling sheet looks like in practice. Built for an AE going into an enterprise deal where the prospect is evaluating Retell or Vapi alongside ElevenLabs.

ElevenLabs Agents · Enterprise Objection Playbook
For AEs in competitive enterprise evaluations · v1 draft
Internal use only
Objection heard
How to respond
"We're already looking at Retell. They told us they're HIPAA compliant too."
Retell has HIPAA, that's true. Ask them to produce the BAA, the VPC deployment option, and the zero-retention mode documentation in writing before the next call. Then ask if their TTS layer is also HIPAA-covered, or if it routes through a third-party provider under a separate agreement. ElevenLabs owns the full stack: synthesis, orchestration, telephony, and data handling, all under one BAA.
"Vapi is cheaper per minute. Our CFO is going to ask why we'd pay more."
Vapi's per-minute rate looks lower because you're paying for orchestration only. You'll still need a TTS provider (often ElevenLabs anyway), an STT layer, and your own telephony. When you add those up and factor in the engineering time to wire it together and keep it stable, the fully-loaded cost is higher than a single ElevenLabs contract. We can model that math together if it helps the CFO conversation.
"We're not sure an AI agent will sound good enough. Our customers will notice if it sounds robotic."
That concern is exactly right, and it's why Retell and Vapi both route to ElevenLabs TTS when they want enterprise calls to sound human. Ask them to demo a call on their default voice stack, then on ElevenLabs. The difference ends the conversation. We can set up that comparison in under 20 minutes.
"We need this integrated with Salesforce and ServiceNow. Can you actually do that?"
Native connectors, not webhooks. After every call, the agent writes a call summary, updates the contact record, and logs disposition in Salesforce automatically. ServiceNow tickets get created or updated in real time. We can walk through the exact integration architecture with your engineering team, and we have a production reference from a current enterprise customer using the same stack.
"We had a bad experience with an AI vendor before. They promised a lot and the pilot didn't deliver."
Don't push back on this. Ask what specifically failed: was it voice quality, integration complexity, call containment rates, or something else? Then commit to a pilot scoped around exactly that dimension, with agreed success criteria in writing before it starts. A pilot where both sides define "success" before it runs is a different thing than a proof-of-concept demo.
"We want to wait until the AI voice agent market matures a bit more."
The companies that waited for the cloud to "mature" in 2012 watched their competitors cut infrastructure costs by half before they moved. The voice AI market is consolidating now, not later. Deutsche Telekom and Revolut didn't wait. If anything, running a scoped pilot this quarter means you're buying at a price point and a level of seller attention that gets harder to get once the category is fully mainstream.
